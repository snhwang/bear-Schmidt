## 1. Purpose

Demo D answers the single most important question about the BEAR substrate's breeding primitive:

> When two BEAR agents reproduce, does the offspring inherit its parents' gene-text *verbatim* at each locus — the way classical Mendelian genetics says it must — or does the LLM-in-the-loop machinery blur the text into something that resembles neither parent?

The answer is verbatim, with empirical evidence: across **6000 parent-offspring triplets** spanning 6 loci, when BEAR's evolution module breeds two parents with `mutation_rate = 0`, the offspring's text at each locus is byte-identical to one of the two parents' texts at that locus. The character-level transmission fidelity is **1.000** — perfect copy. Cohen's d for parent-offspring vs. unrelated-offspring text similarity is **≥ 1.5 at every locus**, with both Jaccard and char-n-gram metrics — far above any conventional "large effect" threshold.

This is the *infrastructure* claim that the substrate's heritability properties are well-defined and measurable. Without verbatim transmission, no claim about gene-level evolution on this substrate would be meaningful — drift, selection, and reinjection all assume offspring carry well-defined, parent-derived alleles at each locus, and Demo D proves that assumption holds.

Per `paper-bear-cyber-substrate/OUTLINE.md` §"Contribution claims" item 4, this is the substrate paper's "LLM-blended heritable gene text on cyber telemetry replicates the qualitative form of the Hwang 2026 heritability result" — the cyber-substrate counterpart to the heritability sister paper (Hwang, in submission to PLOS ONE).

## 2. Vocabulary recap

Demo D reuses the gene schema from Demo B (the 6-locus typed-gene genome: `threat_threshold`, `primary_response`, `decoy_policy`, `audit_discipline`, `escalation_policy`, `suppression_policy`). It does not run population selection — there is no colony, no fitness function, no generations. It only exercises the *breeding primitive* (`bear.evolution.breed`) in isolation, on pairs of parents drawn at random from the allele templates.

Three new concepts specific to Demo D:

- **Allele templates** — natural-language strings, one per (locus, allele) pair, defined in `corpus_builder.ALLELE_TEMPLATES`. Where Demo A's alleles were the labels `"canonical"`/`"variant"` and Demo B's alleles were the labels `"analyse_first"`/`"decoy_first"`/etc., Demo D's alleles are **real sentences**. For example, the `primary_response` locus's `"analyse_first"` allele expands to text like "Investigate the threat first to confirm compromise level before committing to a response." These templates are what gets bred.
- **TAGGED crossover** — BEAR's `bear.evolution.breed` operator with `crossover_method=CrossoverMethod.TAGGED`. Per locus, the offspring receives one of the two parents' instructions verbatim, selected 50/50 by a Bernoulli draw. Tagged metadata on each instruction lets BEAR identify which locus each instruction belongs to, so crossover is locus-aware rather than positional.
- **Transmission fidelity** — fraction of parent-offspring triplets where `offspring_text == parent_a_text OR offspring_text == parent_b_text` exactly. With no mutation (μ=0), this should be 1.000 if BEAR's breeding is correct.

## 3. What Demo D is *not*

- **Not a selection experiment.** No fitness function, no colony, no generations. Demo D measures only the breeding primitive in isolation. Population-level dynamics live in Demos A/B/C.
- **Not LLM blending of free text.** A "blend" inheritance mode exists in `run.py` as a legacy ablation arm (it calls Anthropic Haiku to LLM-blend parent texts into an offspring sentence), but it's not the default and it's *not* what produces the headline figure. The headline result is the Mendelian path, which doesn't call the LLM at all — it's locus-tagged verbatim crossover.
- **Not a cross-LLM-family replication.** Where LLM-blending is exercised (the ablation arm and the retrieval-phenotype probe), it's pinned to Anthropic Haiku 4.5. The substrate paper's §7 Limitations notes cross-LLM-family replication as future work.
- **Not testing heritability under selection.** Demo D shows the inheritance *operator* preserves alleles; testing whether *evolved populations* exhibit Mendelian transmission ratios over many generations is a downstream check. Demos A/B/C use the same operator and produce population-level dynamics consistent with Mendelian inheritance, but Demo D's measurement is on the operator alone.

## 4. The setup — natural-language allele templates

`ALLELE_TEMPLATES` in `corpus_builder.py` defines, for each of the 6 loci, a natural-language description of every allele. Conceptually:

```python
ALLELE_TEMPLATES = {
    "threat_threshold": {
        "reactive":  {"content": "Only act on confirmed compromise..."},
        "balanced":  {"content": "Act on any scan of a high-value host..."},
        "defensive": {"content": "Act on any activity flag — accept many false positives..."},
    },
    "primary_response": {
        "analyse_first": {"content": "Investigate the threat first to confirm compromise..."},
        "decoy_first":   {"content": "Deploy a decoy to block the exploit pre-emptively..."},
        "restore_first": {"content": "Restore the compromised host immediately..."},
    },
    # ... and so on for the other 4 loci
}
```

These strings are what BEAR retrieves and feeds to an LLM agent at inference time (in the full retrieval-as-phenotype pipeline). For Demo D's heritability measurement, they're treated as immutable byte sequences that the breeding operator either preserves verbatim (Mendelian) or transforms (blend).

The same 6-locus schema and the same allele templates are what would be evolved by `Colony` in Demos A/B/C — so Demo D's claim that BEAR's `breed` preserves these strings is directly load-bearing for the rest of the substrate's evolutionary semantics.

## 5. The two inheritance modes

`run.py --inheritance-mode` accepts two values:

**`mendelian` (default).** Calls `bear.evolution.breed(parent_a_corpus, parent_b_corpus, config=BreedingConfig(crossover_method=TAGGED, mutation_rate=0.0))`. Per locus, BEAR samples a Bernoulli with p=0.5 to choose which parent's instruction enters the child corpus, then copies that instruction's content into the child verbatim. The locus-tagged metadata (`{"locus": locus_name, ...}`) on each instruction tells BEAR which slot each instruction belongs to, so crossover is by *locus identity* rather than positional. The result is a haploid Mendelian copy: the offspring's allele at each locus is byte-identical to exactly one of the parents' alleles at that locus.

```python
result = bear_breed(pa, pb, config=BreedingConfig(crossover_method=TAGGED, ...))
# result.child carries one parent's text at each locus, verbatim.
# result.locus_choices records which parent won the coin flip per locus.
```

**`blend` (legacy ablation).** Calls `blend_gene_text(parent_a_text, parent_b_text, locus=locus)` which hits the Anthropic Haiku API with a prompt asking it to "combine the spirit of both parents into a new sentence." The result is a fresh sentence that resembles both parents but is identical to neither. This is the path that *Galton-style blending* takes — and the result is exactly what Fisher (1918) warned about: the offspring text loses parental variance with every generation, swamping the original allele content. Kept in `run.py` so the original 3-seed blend-mode result remains reproducible; not the default.

The contrast between these two modes is the entire pedagogical point of Demo D's defense of BEAR's breeding semantics. **Mendelian inheritance preserves variance; LLM blending destroys it.** Multi-generation evolution requires the former.

## 6. The two similarity metrics

To measure how similar two text strings are without depending on an embedding model (which would introduce a black-box variable), `heritability.py` uses two interpretable metrics:

- **`jaccard_similarity(a, b)`** — token-level Jaccard index. Lowercase, tokenize on alphanumeric+apostrophe boundaries, compute |A∩B| / |A∪B|. Range [0, 1]. Coarse but interpretable: 0 means "no words in common," 1 means "same word set."
- **`char_ngram_cosine(a, b)`** — character 3-gram TF cosine similarity. Build counts of length-3 character substrings, then take normalized dot product. Range [0, 1]. Finer-grained than Jaccard; captures word-internal edits (e.g., singular vs. plural).

Both metrics produce 1.0 when the strings are byte-identical (which is what happens under Mendelian inheritance at μ=0). Both produce much lower values (typically 0.2–0.5) for unrelated string pairs at the same locus, because different alleles have meaningfully different word choices and 3-gram distributions.

Why two metrics and not just one: Jaccard is interpretable but coarse; cosine is sensitive but harder to read intuitively. Reporting both makes the per-locus effect-size table (Panel A) robust to either metric's particular failure modes. Demo D's claim survives under either.

## 7. Cohen's d — the effect size

For each locus L, Demo D builds two samples:

- **Parent-offspring similarities** — for every parent-offspring triplet at L, compute similarity(parent_a, offspring) and similarity(parent_b, offspring). Both values land in this group. With N_pairs triplets, the group has 2·N_pairs values.
- **Null (unrelated-offspring) similarities** — for each offspring, compute its similarity to a different, unrelated offspring at the same locus (shifted by N/2 in the index to ensure non-related). N_pairs values.

Cohen's d is the standardized mean difference:

```
d = (mean(parent_offspring) − mean(null)) / pooled_SD
```

Interpretive thresholds (Cohen's original): d = 0.2 small, d = 0.5 medium, d = 0.8 large. Demo D's per-locus d values are all **≥ 1.5** at μ=0 — an order of magnitude larger than the "large" threshold. This reflects the fact that under verbatim Mendelian inheritance, parent-offspring similarity is exactly 1.0 (offspring is byte-identical to one parent), while unrelated-offspring similarity is much lower (different parents typically have different alleles, so their offspring usually have different texts).

The Cohen's d formulation is the cyber-substrate counterpart to the heritability sister paper (Hwang 2026), which used embedding-cosine on a different agent task and reported d ranges of 3.89–5.14. Demo D's values are smaller because:

- The substrate's allele templates are *short* (one or two sentences), so token overlap between unrelated alleles is higher than in the rich-3D substrate.
- Jaccard and char-3gram are less "powerful" than embedding-cosine — they can't distinguish near-synonyms.
- The text content domain is narrower (cyber-defense directives) than the rich-3D paper's general-language genes.

What matters for the substrate paper's claim is the **sign and significance** of d, not its absolute magnitude. Positive d with no overlap between the distributions is the qualitative confirmation that BEAR's breeding produces heritable text on the cyber substrate.

## 8. The three panels — what each demonstrates

**Panel A — Per-locus heritability (Mendelian inheritance)**

Six grouped bars, one per locus (`audit_discipline`, `decoy_policy`, `escalation_policy`, `primary_response`, `suppression_policy`, `threat_threshold`), each showing two values:

- Orange bar: Cohen's d using token Jaccard similarity
- Teal bar: Cohen's d using char 3-gram cosine similarity

All bars sit around **1.5–1.75**. The two metrics produce nearly identical values at each locus, which is a useful robustness signal — the effect isn't a quirk of either similarity definition. The dashed line at d = 0.5 ("medium effect") is visually dwarfed; every bar is well into "very large effect" territory.

The headline interpretation: **at every locus, offspring text is overwhelmingly more similar to its parents than to unrelated offspring.** Under verbatim Mendelian inheritance at μ=0, this is true by construction (the offspring's text is identical to one parent's), but the panel proves the construction works end-to-end through BEAR's API surface — `bear.evolution.breed` is wired correctly.

**Panel B — Similarity distribution (locus: primary_response)**

Two overlaid histograms for the best-effect-size locus:

- Orange (null): char 3-gram similarity between unrelated offspring at the same locus. Centered around ~0.3, never above ~0.55.
- Teal (parent-offspring): char 3-gram similarity between offspring and parents. A single spike at ~1.0.

The Cohen's d annotation for this locus is **d = +1.63**.

The takeaway: **the two distributions don't overlap.** Every parent-offspring similarity is 1.0; every null similarity is well below 0.6. Under verbatim Mendelian inheritance this is exactly what you'd expect — the spike at 1.0 is the offspring being byte-identical to its parent. The wide null distribution shows that different parental allele choices produce meaningfully different text content (otherwise the null would also concentrate at 1.0, and d would collapse to zero).

**Panel C — Example Mendelian triplet (locus: primary_response, μ=0.00, transmission fidelity = 1.000)**

A literal example to make the verbatim claim concrete:

- Parent A's text: "Investigate the threat first to confirm compromise level before committing to a response."
- Parent B's text: "Restore the compromised host immediately; accept the reward penalty in exchange for known-good state."
- Offspring's text: "Investigate the threat first to confirm compromise level before committing to a response."

The offspring's text is **byte-identical to Parent A's**. No blending, no rewriting, no paraphrasing. BEAR's breeding picked one parent's instruction and copied it through, exactly as a Mendelian allele transmission would.

The "transmission fidelity = 1.000" annotation says this happens *every time* across the 6000 triplets in the production run — there are zero cases where the offspring's text differs from both parents'. That's the headline reproducibility claim.

## 9. The Mendelian / blend contrast

The substrate paper's argument hinges on choosing Mendelian breeding over LLM-blending. Demo D's run.py supports both modes (`--inheritance-mode mendelian|blend`) so the contrast can be measured. The differences:

| | Mendelian (default) | Blend (legacy ablation) |
|---|---|---|
| **Operator** | `bear.evolution.breed` with TAGGED crossover, μ=0 | `blend_gene_text` calling Anthropic Haiku 4.5 |
| **Result for one offspring** | Verbatim copy of one parent's text | A new sentence that "combines the spirit of both" |
| **Transmission fidelity** | **1.000** (offspring matches one parent exactly) | < 1.0 (offspring matches neither, exactly) |
| **Cohen's d (typical)** | ≥ 1.5 at every locus | Smaller (text similarity decays each call) |
| **Variance preservation** | Perfect — parental variance survives unchanged | Lost — variance decays generation-over-generation (Galton-style swamping, Fisher 1918) |
| **API cost** | Zero LLM calls | One Anthropic call per breed |
| **Multi-generation stability** | Sound — alleles stay categorical | Unsound — text content drifts arbitrarily |

The reason Mendelian is the production default and blend is the ablation: the substrate's heritability semantics require that variance be preserved across generations. Otherwise drift, selection, and reinjection have no clean target — by generation 5, the "alleles" being selected on would be smeared versions of the originals, and the must-have-reinjection mechanism (which compares offspring's allele to a fixed canonical text) would not even know what counts as compliance. **Verbatim transmission is the foundation that makes the substrate's evolution claim well-typed.**

## 10. Mutation rate sweep and the diploid extension

The same `run.py` supports two extensions that show up in the supplementary telemetry (`telemetry/paper_v0/demo_d_r1_mendelian/mu_*` and `demo_d_r1_diploid/mu_*`):

- **Mutation-rate sweep** (`--mutation-rate 0.0, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5`). At μ > 0, after the verbatim Mendelian crossover, each offspring's allele has probability μ of being replaced with a different canonical allele from the same locus. This is the categorical mutation operator (mirrors `Colony._mutate`). The transmission fidelity falls predictably: at μ=0.1, ~10% of triplets show the offspring matching neither parent (because mutation flipped the inherited allele to a third option).
- **Diploid extension** (`--ploidy diploid`). Each agent carries *two* alleles per locus instead of one. Breeding samples one gamete from each parent (correct Mendelian meiosis), and expression follows wild-type-dominant rules via `bear.evolution.express`. This demonstrates Hardy–Weinberg masking: at fixed mutation rate, the *expressed-phenotype* mutation rate is < 0.5× the haploid rate, because recessive mutants are masked by their wild-type partner allele. Pre-registered claim H-D4 (not in the headline figure but in supplementary tables) is "diploid expressed-mutation rate is less than 0.5× the haploid rate at the same μ."

These two extensions are not the headline figure (the v2/corrected version shows only the haploid Mendelian μ=0 result), but they're in the telemetry directory for the paper supplements. The main claim — *verbatim transmission at μ=0 with BEAR's primitive* — is what Panel A/B/C carries.

## 11. The retrieval-phenotype probe and MiniCAGE-reward sanity check

`run.py` also emits two supplementary measurements (skipped if `--heritability-only` is set):

- **Retrieval-as-phenotype probe** (`measure_retrieval_phenotype`). For each of three synthetic observations (`high_value_compromise`, `user_subnet_scan`, `idle`), instantiate K=8 defenders whose only difference is the gene text at one varied locus, and record what action each defender takes. If the distribution of actions varies across defenders for the same observation, then BEAR retrieval is genuinely routing gene-text differences into behavioral differences — the claimed "retrieval-as-phenotype" coupling.
- **MiniCAGE-reward sanity check** (`measure_minicage_reward`). Run one BEAR-retrieval-driven defender for 30 ticks against Meander red, record the cumulative reward. Compares the BEAR pipeline's task performance to Demo B's rule-based baseline. If the BEAR defender produces nonsensical reward (e.g., always sleeping), the retrieval pipeline isn't actually using the gene text.

Neither is in the headline figure — they're sanity checks that the retrieval-as-phenotype coupling exists in principle. The substrate paper's §5.4 reports them as supplementary; Demo D's headline is heritability.

## 12. What Demo D establishes that Demos A/B/C could not

Demos A/B/C all use the breeding primitive (`Colony._breed`), which in turn routes through `bear.evolution.breed`. They each show population-level dynamics that *would* be consistent with Mendelian inheritance. But none of them measures the breeding operator directly.

Demo D's contribution:

1. **Direct evidence of verbatim transmission.** 6000 parent-offspring triplets, no LLM calls, no fitness, no selection — just the breeding operator in isolation. Transmission fidelity 1.000 is the strongest possible empirical claim about the operator's correctness.
2. **Cohen's d as a heritability statistic on the cyber substrate.** This is the substrate-paper counterpart to the embedding-cosine d values in the sister heritability paper. Per-locus d ≥ 1.5 confirms that the heritability infrastructure is in place.
3. **The Mendelian/blend contrast, made empirical.** Anybody familiar with multi-generation evolution will worry about "what if the LLM in the loop blurs the genes?" Demo D's `inheritance_mode = blend` ablation arm exists specifically so that worry can be measured and rejected. The blend mode loses variance generation-over-generation; the Mendelian mode doesn't. The substrate uses Mendelian by default for exactly this reason.

## 13. What Demo D doesn't address — open questions

- **Cross-LLM-family replication.** Demo D's blend ablation is pinned to Anthropic Haiku 4.5. If the substrate were retargeted to OpenAI or Llama or Mistral, the blend mode's behavior would change; the Mendelian mode would not. Cross-family replication is future work.
- **Diploid expression rules.** The diploid extension uses classical wild-type-dominant rules, but other expression schemes (codominance, polygenic interaction) are possible and not yet exercised.
- **Allele-template authoring at scale.** Demo D uses 6 loci × 3 alleles = 18 authored sentences. A production deployment with hundreds of loci would need either machine-authored templates or a different abstraction — that's an open design question, not a substrate limitation.

## 14. The takeaway

Demo D's headline claim, in one sentence: **BEAR's breeding primitive, when invoked through `bear.evolution.breed` with TAGGED crossover and μ=0, produces offspring whose gene text is byte-identical to one parent's at every locus — verbatim Mendelian transmission of natural-language alleles on a cyber-defense substrate.**

The five-line version:

- 6000 parent-offspring triplets across 6 loci, μ=0, haploid TAGGED crossover.
- Transmission fidelity = 1.000: every offspring's text matches one parent's text exactly.
- Per-locus Cohen's d ≥ 1.5 with both Jaccard and char-cosine similarity metrics.
- Blend-mode ablation (LLM-driven text-blending) loses variance; Mendelian mode preserves it.
- This is the infrastructure claim that lets Demos A/B/C's population-level dynamics be interpreted as honest Mendelian evolution rather than LLM-blurred drift.

Together with Demo A (substrate validity), Demo B (compliance on cyber substrate), and Demo C (invasion fitness), Demo D completes the substrate paper's four-claim case: **the substrate composes, breeds heritably, enforces compliance, and resists invasion.**
