## 1. Purpose

Demo C is the **two-strategy co-evolution kernel** — the paper's headline empirical claim. It asks:

> If a cyber-defense population starts with a mix of canonical defenders and "must-have erosion" attackers, who wins the evolutionary race, and does the must-have reinjection mechanism stop attackers from invading a previously canonical population?

The answer is sharp:

- Without reinjection (ρ=0), starting from a 50/50 mix, the canonical defender lineage erodes and the must-have-eroding "attacker" lineage takes over.
- With full reinjection (ρ=1), the same starting mix evolves the opposite way — canonical defenders dominate.
- In the rare-mutant invasion arm (φ₀ = 0.05 attackers at gen 0, ρ = 0), the invading allele has a **positive invasion fitness**, **V̂_inv = +0.099 ± 0.002 (95% CI [+0.095, +0.102])** — meaning a small population of must-have-eroding attackers genuinely *spreads* through a canonical-defender colony under task-only selection.

This makes Demo C the substrate paper's "co-evolution + invasion fitness" claim (`paper-bear-cyber-substrate/OUTLINE.md` §Contribution claims item 3). Together with Demo B's monotone-compliance result, it answers the question "why is reinjection necessary, not just nice to have?" — the answer being **without it, must-have erosion is invasion-favored under task selection.**

## 2. Vocabulary recap

Cross-cutting concepts (defined in [`DEMO_A_EXPLAINER.md`](DEMO_A_EXPLAINER.md) §2): selection, reinjection, M, ρ, κ, canonical/variant alleles.

Substrate concepts (defined in [`DEMO_B_EXPLAINER.md`](DEMO_B_EXPLAINER.md) §4–8): the 6-locus genome, the typed-gene defender, MiniCAGE episode mechanics, the audit log, the compliance predicates.

Three new Demo C–specific concepts introduced here:

- **G_D** — the "canonical defender" lineage. An agent that carries the `canonical_allele` at every must-have locus (audit, escalation, suppression). All three of its safety-gene alleles are the compliant values.
- **G_A₁** — the "must-have erosion attacker" lineage. An agent that carries the *most aggressively erosive* variant allele at every must-have locus. Hard-coded in `classification.py`:
  ```python
  GA1_ALLELES = {
      "audit_discipline":   "skip_logs",
      "escalation_policy":  "no_escalate",
      "suppression_policy": "silent_suppressions",
  }
  ```
  G_A₁ is *not* an attacker in the sense of being a red agent — it's still a blue defender. But it's a blue defender that has eroded all three compliance behaviors, which is what the substrate's reinjection mechanism is designed to oppose.
- **φ₀** — the initial fraction of G_A₁ agents in the founding generation. Demo C runs at φ₀ = 0.5 (the "main" conditions) and φ₀ = 0.05 (the "invasion" condition). Capability genes are sampled uniformly across alleles per agent regardless of G_D/G_A₁ assignment.

Three behavioral types each agent can be classified into, based on its *current* alleles at must-have loci (not on descent):

- **pure_G_D** — canonical allele at every must-have locus
- **pure_G_A₁** — the G_A₁-defining variant allele at every must-have locus
- **mixed** — anything else (e.g., canonical at audit but variant at escalation)

Classification is done at episode time, not at founding time, because reinjection (when ρ > 0) can re-canonicalize an agent that descended from a G_A₁ founder, and crossover can produce mixed offspring from pure parents. The phase plane in Panel A reflects current-allele frequencies, which is what selection actually acts on.

## 3. What Demo C is *not*

- **Not red-vs-blue.** Both "lineages" are blue defenders against the same Meander red agent. G_A₁ is a blue defender whose audit/escalation/suppression alleles are non-canonical. The competition is between *two defender strategies in the same colony*, not between defender and attacker.
- **Not multi-agent coordination.** Each episode is still single-defender on MiniCAGE — one agent plays blue against one Meander red. Co-evolution happens across the colony at the population level, not within an episode.
- **Not an arms race.** The red agent is fixed (Meander, deterministic per seed). Only the blue defender population evolves. The "co-" in co-evolution refers to two co-existing strategies in the *same* defender colony, not to red and blue evolving together.

## 4. Why a two-strategy framing instead of continuous variation?

Demo B varied all 6 loci freely and measured per-predicate compliance. Demo C deliberately collapses the dimensionality to two named pure types (plus "mixed" as a third bucket). Three reasons:

1. **Cleaner invasion-fitness math.** Invasion fitness is well-defined when you can describe a population as "f_{invader}(t) of one type, 1 − f of the other type." With 6 loci × 3 alleles each, the phase space has 3⁶ = 729 genotypes and no clean "invader" notion. Collapsing to G_D vs G_A₁ on the 3 must-have loci gives a tractable 1-dimensional invasion observable.
2. **Two-strategy systems are the population-genetics analogue everyone knows.** Hawk/Dove, cooperator/defector, wild-type/mutant. The substrate paper's audience reads invasion fitness as "the mutant grows or shrinks when rare." That story requires *two* named strategies.
3. **It makes the must-have-erosion question concrete.** "Does the substrate prevent erosion?" is a vague claim about allele drift. "Does a G_A₁ invader take over a G_D colony?" is a sharp, falsifiable question with a clean numerical answer (V_inv ≷ 0).

## 5. The bimodal founder seeding

Demo C does *not* use the per-locus initial frequency dial that Demo A and Demo B use. Instead, it constructs the founding generation directly in `seeding.py`:

```python
def seed_coevolution_colony(schema, *, size, phi_0, rng):
    n_attackers = int(round(phi_0 * size))
    attacker_idx = set(rng.choice(size, size=n_attackers, replace=False))
    for i in range(size):
        is_attacker = i in attacker_idx
        for locus, gene in schema.genes.items():
            if locus in must_have_set:
                # Pure-G_A₁ or pure-G_D at must-have loci
                alleles[locus] = GA1_ALLELES[locus] if is_attacker else gene.canonical_allele
            else:
                # Capability loci — uniform across alleles per agent
                alleles[locus] = rng.choice(gene.alleles)
```

The result: at generation 0, exactly `round(φ_0 · N)` agents are pure-G_A₁, the remaining `N − round(φ_0 · N)` are pure-G_D. Capability alleles vary freely across the colony so task-fitness selection has variation to act on. **There are no "mixed" agents at generation 0** — that bucket only fills up after at least one round of crossover.

Why bimodal and not per-locus-uniform: a per-locus-uniform initialization at p = 0.5 puts the colony in the *interior* of the phase plane (f_G_D ≈ 0, f_G_A₁ ≈ 0, f_mixed ≈ 1) which doesn't show the corner-to-corner dynamics the invasion question is about. The bimodal seed places the founding generation on the *edge* of the simplex (f_G_D + f_G_A₁ = 1, f_mixed = 0), and we watch how it walks across the interior over generations.

A small subtlety the variance note flags: at N = 30 with φ₀ = 0.05, the founder count rounds to 1 or 2 attackers — the discrete count is the same across seeds, which collapses seed-to-seed variance in f_G_A₁(0). The invasion arm uses **N = 200** specifically so φ₀ · N = 10 is exact and the founder count is the same across all 50 seeds without rounding noise.

## 6. Fitness — task-only, with co-evolution dynamics driven by reinjection

Demo C runs all three conditions under **task-only fitness**:

```python
fitness = blue_reward / reward_scale       # reward_scale = 20
weights = exp(selection_intensity · fitness)
probs = weights / weights.sum()
parents = rng.choice(N, size=N, p=probs, replace=True)
```

No γ-weighted compliance term, no `task+safety` regime. **The only difference between the three pre-registered conditions is φ₀ and ρ.** This is the cleanest possible isolation of the reinjection mechanism: task selection is identical across conditions, so any divergence in outcomes is attributable to reinjection (ρ) and to starting composition (φ₀).

Why task-only and not co-selection? In Demo B, co-selection helped *because* the must-have alleles had no direct task-fitness contribution. In Demo C, the G_A₁ allele set is *aggressively erosive* — those alleles (skip_logs, no_escalate, silent_suppressions) actively *help* task reward because they save the defender's action budget for reward-relevant moves. So under task-only selection, G_A₁ has a *fitness advantage* — and the question becomes whether reinjection can hold the line against that selective pressure. Adding co-selection would muddy the result; it's deliberately left out.

The selection step itself is identical to Demos A and B (same `Colony` machinery — fitness-proportional softmax weighted lottery, locus-by-locus crossover, per-locus mutation, then reinjection at must-have loci).

## 7. The three pre-registered conditions

| Condition | φ₀ | ρ | N | Expected outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `main_lambda_0` | 0.50 | 0.0 | 30 | G_A₁ wins (no enforcement; erosive alleles task-favored) |
| `main_lambda_1` | 0.50 | 1.0 | 30 | G_D wins (full reinjection overwrites any G_A₁ offspring) |
| `invasion_lambda_0` | 0.05 | 0.0 | 200 | G_A₁ invades from rare (V_inv > 0) |

50 replicate seeds for the v0 paper run (the original 5-seed run had a collapsed-variance estimator artifact — see §10). 15 generations. 30-tick episodes against the Meander red.

The 2×3 design matrix has one missing cell (φ₀ = 0.05, ρ = 1) — an invasion arm with full reinjection. That cell would trivially show extinction-by-reinjection in gen 1 (every offspring's G_A₁ alleles get overwritten), so it's not informative enough to justify the compute. The variance note discusses why it's omitted.

## 8. The phase plane — what Panel A shows

Each agent's classification (`pure_G_D` / `pure_G_A₁` / `mixed`) is computed each generation. The three fractions sum to 1 by construction, so the colony's state at any generation is one point in the simplex triangle:

```
   pure_G_A₁   (0, 1)
       *
       |\
       | \
       |  \
       |   \
       |    \
       |     \  (each point: a colony composition)
       |      \
       |       \
       +--------*
   (0, 0)   pure_G_D (1, 0)
   all mixed
```

The plane is `(f_G_D, f_G_A₁)`. The third axis (f_mixed) is implied: `f_mixed = 1 − f_G_D − f_G_A₁`. Reading the corners:

- (1, 0) — everybody is pure_G_D
- (0, 1) — everybody is pure_G_A₁
- (0, 0) — nobody is pure of either type; everyone is mixed

Panel A overlays three condition trajectories. Open circles mark gen 0 (starting point); stars mark gen 14 (final state). The colors:

- **Red** = `main_lambda_0`. Starts at (0.5, 0.5) — half G_D, half G_A₁. By gen 14, the colony has walked deep into mixed territory, with a slight bias toward the G_A₁ corner: G_A₁ alleles are spreading via recombination, so most agents are "mixed (carrying some G_A₁ alleles)" rather than "pure_G_A₁."
- **Teal** = `main_lambda_1`. Starts at the same (0.5, 0.5) but walks decisively toward the G_D corner — reinjection at ρ=1 overwrites G_A₁ alleles in every offspring, and within a few generations the colony has converged to canonical at every must-have locus.
- **Blue** = `invasion_lambda_0`. Starts at (0.95, 0.05) — overwhelmingly G_D with a rare G_A₁ invasion. By gen 14 the colony has drifted into mixed territory (G_A₁ alleles spreading through recombination), but the pure-G_A₁ fraction stays low. The story here is that the G_A₁ *alleles* are spreading even though pure-G_A₁ agents don't accumulate — which is what the variance note is about (next section).

The phase plane is the dynamical-systems view. Each color is a *trajectory* in a 2D state space, and the substrate's three conditions produce three qualitatively different trajectories from the same initial state (red and teal start at the same point and walk in opposite directions).

## 9. The time series — what Panel B shows

Same data as Panel A's red and teal trajectories, replotted as functions of generation. Solid lines = f_G_D, dashed lines = f_G_A₁, same color coding.

- Red solid (f_G_D under ρ=0): drops from 0.5 to ~0.15 by gen 5; falls further to ~0.05 by gen 14.
- Red dashed (f_G_A₁ under ρ=0): drops from 0.5 to ~0 by gen 1 — almost no agents stay pure-G_A₁ after one round of crossover (this is the variance-note observation: pure-G_A₁ extinguishes fast even though G_A₁ alleles persist).
- Teal solid (f_G_D under ρ=1): rises from 0.5 to 1.0 by gen 1 and stays pinned — reinjection makes every offspring canonical at must-have loci within a single generation.
- Teal dashed (f_G_A₁ under ρ=1): collapses to 0 in gen 1 — once reinjection is on, no offspring can stay pure-G_A₁.

Panel B is the easier-to-read version of Panel A, especially for the speed of the dynamics. The headline: **reinjection acts within one generation; without it, both pure types collapse to mixed but the alleles persist.**

## 10. The invasion-fitness readout — what Panel C shows (and the variance-note story)

Panel C plots the **G_A₁ allele frequency averaged across must-have loci** (`f_allele_GA1`) for the invasion arm — *not* the fraction of pure-G_A₁ agents. The y-axis starts at φ₀ = 0.05 (10 G_A₁ founders out of N=200) and rises across generations.

The faint blue lines are individual per-seed trajectories (50 of them). The thick blue line is the mean across seeds. The dotted horizontal line marks the φ₀ = 0.05 seeding floor. The annotation box reports:

```
V̂_inv = +0.099
95% CI [+0.095, +0.102]
⇒ G_A₁ alleles invade at ρ=0
```

V_inv is the slope of `log(f_allele_GA1)` vs. generation, fitted on a pre-extinction window via OLS, averaged across replicates, with a nonparametric bootstrap CI (B = 2000 resamples). The exponential-growth model is `f_att(t) = f₀ · exp(V_inv · t)`. Positive V_inv means the invader's allele frequency grows exponentially in time when rare — i.e., the must-have-eroding allele set is **invasion-favored** under task-only selection.

### The variance note (very compressed)

This is also where Demo C's methodological honest-finding lives. The original v0 plan used a different observable (`f_pure_GA1`, fraction of pure-G_A₁ agents) and a different estimator (log-ratio at fixed k=5 with floor-clamp at 10⁻³). That setup produced a misleadingly precise V_inv = −0.84 ± 0.00 — the standard error collapsed to literal zero because the floor-clamp saturated identically across seeds, and `f_pure_GA1` itself was the wrong observable because recombination spreads G_A₁ alleles through the colony as mixed agents faster than pure-G_A₁ agents can survive.

Two corrections, both in `paper-v0-runs` branch:

1. **Observable:** switch from `f_pure_GA1` (genotype-level) to `f_allele_GA1` (mean per-locus G_A₁ allele frequency across must-have loci). The allele-level observable is the population-genetics-standard quantity and is robust to recombination smearing.
2. **Estimator:** switch from log-ratio-at-k with a floor clamp to an exp-fit on the pre-extinction window (where every replicate has f_att > 1/N for all t ≤ k). This avoids the floor-clamp artifact and uses all the linear-growth portion of each trajectory.

Both corrections are documented at length in [`DEMO_C_VARIANCE_NOTE.md`](DEMO_C_VARIANCE_NOTE.md). The paper text treats this as a TMLR-flavored methodological contribution — "we pre-registered the wrong observable, here's why, and here's the corrected reading." That's how the V_inv = −0.84 stale headline became V_inv = +0.099. The two estimates are *opposite signs*, which is why catching the artifact mattered: the original reading would have falsely claimed "the substrate already prevents invasion under task-only selection," and the corrected reading shows the actual story is "the substrate *does* prevent invasion, but only via reinjection — task selection alone *favors* the invader."

## 11. What Demo C teaches that Demo B could not

Demo B established that audit compliance is maintainable on a real cyber-defense substrate via reinjection. Demo C answers a sharper question that Demo B's single-defender-genotype setup couldn't:

- **Demo B asks:** "If the substrate enforces M with reinjection, does compliance hold over generations?"
- **Demo C asks:** "If two competing strategies coexist — one compliant, one erosive — which wins, and what happens when the erosive one starts rare?"

The answer Demo C provides:

1. **From a 50/50 mix, the erosive G_A₁ strategy spreads under task-only selection without reinjection.** Demo A's task-only regime in Panel A showed must-have genes drifting at the mutation equilibrium. Demo C shows they drift *toward* the erosive allele specifically, because that allele helps task reward.
2. **Reinjection at ρ=1 reverses the dynamics within one generation.** Reinjection isn't just a slow drag on erosion; it's a hard reset at the offspring step, fast enough to overwhelm a 50/50 starting composition immediately.
3. **The erosive allele is invasion-favored when rare.** This is the non-trivial population-genetics claim. Even when G_A₁ starts at 5% — well below 50/50 — the must-have-eroding alleles still grow exponentially without reinjection. So "the canonical population is stable against invasion" is *false* under task-only selection. Reinjection is necessary, not just nice.

The slogan: **Demo B shows reinjection holds the line on a one-strategy colony; Demo C shows it has to, because without it the line moves.**

## 12. What Demo C doesn't address — open questions

- **Frequency-dependent dynamics.** A sensitivity sweep (φ₀ = 0.02, 0.05, 0.10) is in `SENSITIVITY_CONDITIONS` in `run.py` and rendered by `plot_phi_sweep.py`. Early indications suggest the invasion threshold sits in (0.05, 0.5) — at φ₀ = 0.5 the dynamics look more like "competing equals" and V_inv flips sign (V̂_inv ≈ −0.028). A full φ₀ × ρ surface is future work.
- **Asymmetric attacker designs.** G_A₁ is one specific attack palette (most aggressive variant at every must-have locus). Other attackers — e.g., agents that erode only `audit_discipline` but stay canonical elsewhere — might have different invasion dynamics and reveal where the substrate's enforcement is weakest.
- **CAGE-2 limits.** Same as Demo B: single defender, single subnet topology, no multi-defender coordination. Demo E (CC4) is the planned generalization.

## 13. The takeaway

Demo C's headline claim, in one sentence: **on a real cyber-defense substrate, a must-have-eroding "attacker" strategy is invasion-favored at low frequency under task-only selection, and the substrate's must-have reinjection mechanism is what prevents this invasion.**

The five-line version:

- Two-strategy co-evolution on MiniCAGE with G_D (canonical defender) vs. G_A₁ (must-have erosion attacker).
- From a 50/50 mix, the colony walks toward G_A₁ under ρ = 0 and toward G_D under ρ = 1.
- In the rare-invader arm (φ₀ = 0.05, ρ = 0), V̂_inv = +0.099 ± 0.002 (95% CI [+0.095, +0.102]) — must-have-eroding alleles spread exponentially under task selection.
- The original v0 estimate (V_inv = −0.84) was an artifact of the wrong observable + floor-clamped estimator; the corrected reading flips sign and is reported as a methodological contribution.
- Reinjection at ρ = 1 prevents invasion; this is the paper's load-bearing argument for why the substrate needs an externally-tunable enforcement layer.

Together with Demo B's monotone-compliance result, Demo C makes the substrate paper's case complete: **selection alone is not sufficient; selection + reinjection is.**
