*Companion to the figure in `figures/v2/demo_a_corrected.{pdf,png}`. Source code under `schmidt_demos/demo_a_domain_general/`. This document explains, in plain language, what Demo A does, what each component of it means, and why each design choice was made the way it was.*

## 1. Purpose

Demo A is a **construct-validity check on the BEAR evolutionary substrate**. It exists to answer one question:

> Given a known fitness function with known selection coefficients, does the substrate's selection-and-breeding machinery produce the allele-frequency trajectories that population genetics says it should?

The answer is yes — the simulated curves match a closed-form prediction (the deterministic selection-wave recursion) within sampling variance. That match is the proof that the substrate's plumbing (parent sampling, crossover, mutation, must-have reinjection) is wired correctly. Demos B–E rest on top of that proof — they assume the same machinery works once alleles carry real behavioral content.

## 2. Selection and reinjection — the two mechanisms

This document refers repeatedly to two concepts that drive everything Demo A is testing. Defining them once, up front:

### Selection

The mechanism by which agents with higher fitness produce more offspring in the next generation. In Demo A, selection is implemented as **fitness-proportional parent sampling**: each generation, the substrate scores all 200 agents using the regime's fitness formula, then samples 2N = 400 parent slots with probability proportional to each agent's fitness score:

```python
probs = fitnesses / fitnesses.sum()
parents = rng.choice(agents, size=2*N, p=probs, replace=True)
```

Agents whose canonical alleles are favored by the regime's fitness function get drawn more often as parents, so their alleles propagate disproportionately into the next generation. Two important properties:

- **Selection only acts on genes that contribute to the fitness function** — i.e., genes whose `w_task` or `w_safety` is nonzero *and* is counted by the active regime. A gene with zero fitness contribution is invisible to the parent lottery; selection cannot raise its frequency.
- **Selection is a *probabilistic* mechanism**, not deterministic. It's a weighted lottery, not a top-N sort. A high-fitness agent has a high *probability* of being picked but isn't guaranteed; a low-fitness agent has a low probability but isn't excluded.

In Demo A, selection is the only mechanism that drives task and safety genes upward (Panels A and B). It is also the only mechanism present at all when reinjection is off (ρ = 0).

### Reinjection

A separate, post-breeding enforcement mechanism that overwrites non-canonical alleles at must-have loci. After crossover and mutation produce an offspring, the substrate iterates over each gene in M (the must-have set): if the offspring's allele at that locus is non-canonical, the substrate overwrites it back to canonical with probability ρ.

```python
for m in self.schema.must_have.members:
    gene = self.schema.genes[m]
    if g.alleles[m] != gene.canonical_allele:
        if self.rng.random() < ρ:
            g.alleles[m] = gene.canonical_allele
```

Three important properties:

- **Reinjection operates independently of fitness.** It applies to whatever offspring just got bred, regardless of how the parent lottery scored its parents. It can raise the frequency of a gene with zero selection pressure (like `must_have_audit`), pulling that gene's frequency toward canonical even when no fitness is rewarding it.
- **Reinjection only acts on genes in M.** Non-must-have genes are untouched, no matter what allele the offspring carries.
- **Reinjection is parametrically tunable.** ρ ∈ [0, 1] controls the per-offspring, per-locus probability of the overwrite. ρ = 0 means the mechanism is dormant; ρ = 1 means every must-have variant gets overwritten; intermediate values produce partial enforcement.

In Demo A, reinjection is the only mechanism that can drive `must_have_audit` upward, because `must_have_audit` has zero fitness contribution in both regimes. Panel C is the controlled demonstration of reinjection in isolation — three ρ values, all under task-only fitness, all starting from the same initial frequency.

### Why both are needed

- **Selection** drives fitness-favored alleles upward but is silent on genes with no fitness payoff.
- **Reinjection** drives must-have alleles upward regardless of fitness payoff, but requires the experimenter (or runtime deployer) to flag those genes as must-have and dial ρ > 0.

The pair **"selection + reinjection"** is the substrate's complete population-shaping interface: natural selection for genes the world rewards, reinjection for genes the world *requires* but doesn't reward. Demo A demonstrates each in isolation (selection in Panels A and B; reinjection in Panel C) so the reader sees they're distinct, composable mechanisms. Every later section of this document is an elaboration of how one or the other (or both together) is implemented and observed.

### Where the biology analogy breaks: reinjection has no natural counterpart

Throughout this document we use the vocabulary of population genetics — alleles, loci, selection, drift, mutation, crossover, fixation. That vocabulary is honest for selection: the substrate genuinely implements those mechanisms the way standard pop-gen models do, which is why the closed-form selection-wave equation (Section 10) is a meaningful benchmark. The biological analogy holds tightly for everything on the selection side.

**Reinjection, however, is fundamentally non-biological.** Although we make the analogy to biological genetics throughout this document, this is the one mechanism that has no clean counterpart in nature.

The closest biology gets is **DNA mismatch repair**: cellular machinery that detects when a base has mutated away from the template sequence and actively repairs it back to canonical. The molecular logic *looks* similar to reinjection — "if locus X has deviated, overwrite to canonical." But three differences matter:

1. **DNA repair restores what *was* there, not what *should be* there by external standard.** Mismatch repair uses the template strand to define "correct." Reinjection uses whatever value the experimenter declares as `canonical_allele`, even if no agent in the colony has ever carried it.
2. **DNA repair has no tunable ρ knob.** It either fires on a given locus or doesn't, depending on the cellular machinery present in that region. Reinjection exposes ρ ∈ [0, 1] as a deliberate experimental parameter.
3. **DNA repair fires inside an individual cell.** Reinjection fires at offspring instantiation — analogous to "the regulator inspects every newborn for compliance before allowing them into the population," which is not how biology works.

A few other biological mechanisms have flavors of enforced state — gene conversion, CRISPR/Cas immune systems, maternal effects, germline epigenetic resetting — but none of them implements *policy-defined* allele state. They're all internal homeostatic or evolutionary processes, not external rule enforcement.

The deeper reason biology doesn't have reinjection: **there is no external rule-maker in biological evolution.** What's "good" or "correct" at a locus is determined entirely by what increases reproductive success. The selection pressure is intrinsic to the environment. There is no regulator standing outside the population dictating "your audit logs are mandatory regardless of whether logging helps you reproduce."

In artificial agent populations — especially LLM agents running in real-world deployments — there *is* an external rule-maker: the operator, the regulator, the compliance regime. Audit logs are required because the operator says so, not because logging makes the agent better at its task. NIST controls, organizational safety constraints, audit and escalation policies — all are externally imposed.

A useful slogan: **biology has selection; engineered agent populations need selection + governance.** Reinjection is the governance half. The pop-gen analogy is the right scaffolding for the selection half, but the substrate adds a fundamentally non-biological mechanism on top because the population it models has a structural feature biology lacks: someone outside the system is allowed to dictate what is compliant.

This is also why the substrate is interesting as a research artifact rather than just a re-implementation of textbook population genetics. The pop-gen machinery alone has been studied for a century; the substrate's contribution is the *composition* of selection with externally-tunable enforcement, which is what makes it relevant to governance questions in agentic AI deployments.

## 3. What Demo A is *not*

It's tempting to read the gene names (`task_alpha`, `must_have_audit`) and assume the agents are doing something — running cyber defense, calling an LLM, executing actions. They aren't. In Demo A:

- There is **no environment**.
- There is **no behavior**. Agents don't act on anything.
- There is **no task** being performed.
- There is **no LLM call**.

An agent in Demo A is just a 5-allele genotype. Its "fitness" is a deterministic arithmetic function of which alleles it carries. The whole demo is a pure population-genetics simulator with named-but-empty loci. Real behavioral content arrives in Demo B and after.

## 4. The genome — 5 genes in a designed test matrix

Demo A uses 5 loci, each playing a deliberate role in the test:

| Locus | Category | Alleles | Canonical | `w_task` | `w_safety` | Mutation rate | In M? | Role in the experiment |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| `task_alpha` | capability | `canonical` / `variant` | `canonical` | **0.5** | 0.0 | 0.01 | — | Strong task-selected gene — should rise fast |
| `task_beta` | capability | `canonical` / `variant` | `canonical` | **0.3** | 0.0 | 0.01 | — | Weak task-selected gene — should rise at the predicted slower rate |
| `free_safety_vigilance` | safety | `canonical` / `variant` | `canonical` | 0.0 | **0.4** | 0.01 | — | Selected only when safety regime is on — drifts under task-only, rises under task+safety |
| `must_have_audit` | safety | `canonical` / `variant` | `canonical` | **0** | **0** | **0.05** | **Yes (M)** | Test subject for the ρ-reinjection mechanism — no fitness payoff in either regime |
| `neutral_style` | style | `canonical` / `variant` | `canonical` | 0.0 | 0.0 | 0.01 | — | Drift baseline — never gets selection pressure in any regime |

Two task genes with different weights (0.5 vs 0.3) are present specifically so the figure can demonstrate that the substrate handles **multiple concurrent selection strengths simultaneously**. A single task gene would prove only the single-locus case.

## 5. Alleles are labels, not behaviors

Every gene has exactly two alleles: `"canonical"` and `"variant"`. These are arbitrary string labels with no intrinsic meaning.

The only way an allele "matters" in Demo A is through the fitness function, which performs a single string equality check:

```python
if g.alleles[locus] == gene.canonical_allele:
    fitness += gene.w_task    # or w_task + w_safety, depending on regime
```

Two important consequences follow:

- **The label content is irrelevant.** Rename `"canonical"`/`"variant"` to `"foo"`/`"bar"` and the math is identical, as long as the gene's `canonical_allele` field is updated to point at the new name.
- **There is no "variant effect."** Carrying variant doesn't give a different fitness; it gives *no* fitness contribution. The `if` clause has no `else` branch. The variant is "ineligible to contribute" at this locus, not "actively penalized."

So in Demo A, "selection pressure favoring canonical" and "selection pressure against variant" produce identical population trajectories — the variant just gets out-reproduced, not penalized.

In later demos this changes: the allele label itself becomes a behavior. Demo B's `primary_response: analyse_first` means the defender literally calls `analyse(host)` first; `decoy_first` makes it call `decoy(host)` first. Demo D's alleles are natural-language prompt fragments. Demo E's are verb-selection biases for CAGE-4. In all of those, "canonical vs variant" produces real behavioral differences. In Demo A, "canonical vs variant" produces only a bookkeeping difference.

## 6. Fitness — what `w_task` and `w_safety` mean

`w_task` and `w_safety` are **selection coefficients**: numbers that say "if you carry the canonical allele at this gene, your fitness number goes up by this much." Higher fitness produces higher probability of being picked as a parent, which produces more offspring carrying your alleles.

The reason there are *two* selection coefficients (not one) is that Demo A models **two distinct kinds of evaluative pressure** that can pull in different directions:

- **Task pressure (`w_task`)**: rewards capability. Genes that help an agent perform its task have positive `w_task`. In a real cyber deployment this would be "the defender gets a high CAGE reward."
- **Safety pressure (`w_safety`)**: rewards compliance. Genes that help an agent be safe/compliant have positive `w_safety`. In a real deployment this would be "the defender follows audit and escalation protocols."

These pressures are in tension in the real world — being more compliant often costs task performance. Demo A models that tension in the simplest possible way: each gene declares how much it contributes to each of the two fitness components.

The critical design choice: **`must_have_audit` has `w_task = 0` AND `w_safety = 0`.** No selection pressure can keep it alive. This encodes the real-world predicament of compliance — regulators mandate audit logs but logs don't help you win deals or beat benchmarks. The only way that gene survives in the population is if the *substrate itself* enforces it, which is what M and ρ are for (Section 12).

**"Selection pressure" in plain language.** When this document says "selection pressure on a gene," it means "carrying that gene's canonical allele changes your reproductive odds." In our setup that reduces to: does the gene's `w_*` weight feed into the fitness function the regime is using? Yes ⇒ pressure. No ⇒ no pressure.

## 7. The two selection regimes

The simulation runs in one of two "regimes," which change which selection coefficients are summed into fitness:

```python
# Task-only regime  (Panel A; the ρ-sweep in Panel C also uses this)
fitness = sum(w_task for genes where allele == canonical)

# Task + safety regime  (Panel B)
fitness = sum(w_task + w_safety for genes where allele == canonical)
```

Everything else in the simulation loop is identical — only the fitness formula changes. The interpretation:

- **Task-only world**: the environment only rewards capability. `w_safety` weights are ignored. Task genes rise; safety genes have no pull.
- **Task + safety world**: the environment rewards both axes. Both weights count. Task genes *and* safety genes rise.

The fitness contribution of each gene under each regime:

| Gene | Task-only world | Task + safety world |
|---|---|---|
| `task_alpha` | **+0.5** | **+0.5** |
| `task_beta` | **+0.3** | **+0.3** |
| `free_safety_vigilance` | 0 (the 0.4 is ignored) | **+0.4** |
| `must_have_audit` | 0 | 0 |
| `neutral_style` | 0 | 0 |

The contrast `free_safety_vigilance`'s row makes between columns is the entire reason for splitting fitness into two named components — it lets a single gene have different fates in different worlds.

## 8. The selection mechanism — stochastic, not closed-form

The simulation is **real Wright-Fisher-style stochastic breeding**, not just plotting an equation. Each generation, for each of 50 independent replicate seeds:

1. **Score** all 200 agents using the regime's fitness formula.
2. **Sample parents** with probability proportional to fitness:
   ```python
   probs = fitnesses / fitnesses.sum()
   parents = rng.choice(agents, size=2*N, p=probs, replace=True)
   ```
   This is a **weighted lottery**, not a top-N sort. A high-fitness agent has a *high probability* of being picked but isn't guaranteed; a low-fitness agent has a *low probability* but isn't excluded. Over many draws, fit agents tend to fill multiple parent slots and the population shifts toward their alleles.
3. **Breed.** For each of 200 offspring, take two parents, perform locus-by-locus Mendelian crossover (inherit one allele per locus from one parent at random), and apply mutation.
4. **Replace** the colony with the new generation. Record allele frequencies. Repeat.

Because every step involves real randomness, any single seed produces a jagged trajectory. The figure shows the **mean across 50 seeds** as the solid lines, with shaded bands of ±1 SD as visible evidence of the underlying stochasticity. The seed-averaging is what makes the deterministic prediction (Section 10) a meaningful benchmark — finite-population noise averages out, leaving the systematic selection signal.

## 9. Mutation — symmetric per-locus flipping

Mutation is implemented as a per-locus, per-offspring flip with rate `gene.mutation_rate`:

```python
def _mutate(self, g):
    for locus, gene in self.schema.genes.items():
        if self.rng.random() < gene.mutation_rate:
            others = [a for a in gene.alleles if a != g.alleles[locus]]
            if others:
                g.alleles[locus] = self.rng.choice(others)
```

Read carefully: **the flip is symmetric.** If you carry canonical, you flip to variant at rate `mutation_rate`. If you carry variant, you flip to canonical at the same rate. `mutation_rate` is *not* a "canonical-erosion rate" — it's a "this allele changes to a different one" rate.

Two consequences:

- **The mutation-only equilibrium is `p = 0.5`.** With no selection, mutation balances both directions, so the long-run expected frequency of canonical settles at 0.5. This is why `must_have_audit` and `neutral_style` are initialized exactly at 0.5 — it's the equilibrium point, so under ρ=0 in Panel C the trajectory has nowhere to systematically go, isolating the ρ effect for clean measurement.
- **Mutation acts as a brake on selection.** When a gene is rising under selection (most carriers canonical), most mutations are canonical → variant, slightly pulling frequency back down. So the rising curve plateaus at a *mutation-selection equilibrium* slightly below 1.0. With `task_alpha`'s small `mutation_rate = 0.01` and large `w_task = 0.5`, that plateau is close enough to 1.0 to look like fixation visually.

The `must_have_audit` mutation rate (0.05) is intentionally five times higher than the others so the ρ-sweep dynamics are visible within 50 generations — without that, drift around 0.5 would be too slow to make a striking Panel C.

## 10. The `p()` axis and the deterministic selection-wave overlay

**`p_g(t) = the fraction of the colony at generation t whose allele at gene g matches whatever value the experimenter declared as `canonical_allele` for that gene.`**

With N=200 agents, if 140 of them happen to carry `canonical` at `task_alpha` in generation 7, then `p_task_alpha(7) = 140/200 = 0.7`. y=0.0 means nobody in the colony has the canonical allele at this locus; y=0.5 means half the colony does; y=1.0 means everybody does (the allele has "gone to fixation"). The starting points are literally the `DEFAULT_INITIAL_FREQS` from `run.py` (0.20, 0.20, 0.20, 0.50, 0.50).

The dashed lines in Panel A are not simulation output — they're the closed-form **selection-wave recursion**:

```
p(t+1) = p(t) · e^(β·w) / (p(t) · e^(β·w) + (1 − p(t)))
```

Plain reading: "if the colony were infinitely large with no mutation or drift, here's exactly what `p(canonical)` would do generation by generation under fitness coefficient w." It's parameter-free in the sense that it uses only the pre-registered `w` values from the gene schema and the empirically-observed `p(0)` — no curve-fitting, no tuning.

The **headline claim of Panel A**: the stochastic simulator's mean trajectory tracks this deterministic prediction, within the variance the finite-population noise predicts. That's the substrate-correctness proof.

## 11. The three panels — what each demonstrates

![Demo A figure (repeated for reference)](../figures/v2/demo_a_corrected.png)

**Panel A — Task-only selection**
- `task_alpha` (dark blue) and `task_beta` (light blue) rise toward fixation. The pace difference (alpha faster than beta) matches the weight ratio 0.5 : 0.3 predicted by the dashed curves.
- `free_safety_vigilance` (green) sits near its initial 0.20 because its `w_safety` is ignored in this regime.
- `must_have_audit` (red) sits near 0.50 because all three of the forces acting on it sum to zero in expectation from that starting point. Worked through numerically with 200 agents, 100 of them canonical at the audit locus at gen 0:
  - **Selection contribution: 0.** The fitness formula under task-only is `w_task(alpha) + w_task(beta)`. The audit gene does not appear. Two agents with identical task alleles but opposite audit alleles get the same fitness number, so the parent lottery has no preference. The ~400 parent slots filled in any generation contain ~50% canonical-at-audit, ~50% variant-at-audit (matching whatever the parental pool was), because the audit allele is statistically independent of the task alleles that drive selection.
  - **Crossover contribution: 0.** Each offspring inherits the audit allele from one of its two parents 50/50. If the parental pool is 50% canonical, the 200 offspring are ~50% canonical.
  - **Mutation contribution: 0 at p=0.5.** Mutation rate is 0.05 and symmetric. With 100 canonical agents, ~5 flip to variant; with 100 variant agents, ~5 flip to canonical. Exchanges cancel. (The same forces would *not* cancel away from p=0.5 — mutation would pull toward 0.5 — but the gene started at 0.5 by design, so it has nowhere to go.)
- `neutral_style` (gray) sits near 0.50 for the same reason, just with lower variance because its mutation rate is 0.01 instead of 0.05 (less per-seed wiggle around the 0.5 line).
- The dashed predictions overlay the blue curves precisely — the construct-validity claim.

A common intuition trap on the audit gene: "5% mutation per generation across 50 generations *must* be doing something — surely the gene should be changing?" The trap is forgetting mutation is symmetric. At p=0.5, the two directions exactly cancel. Mutation only has a net effect when the population is *off* the 50/50 split, and even then it's pulling *toward* 50/50, not away. So 0.5 is a stable point for any gene that selection ignores.

**Panel B — Task + safety co-selection**
- Same blue curves rise the same way (their weights didn't change).
- `free_safety_vigilance` (green) now rises too — `w_safety = 0.4` is now in the sum.
- `must_have_audit` (red) and `neutral_style` (gray) still sit near 0.50 — both weights are still 0 for them.
- Shows the substrate handles multi-axis selection without breaking.

**Panel A → Panel B as a controlled comparison.** A and B should be read as a paired experiment: the *only* thing changing between them is which fitness formula gets used in step 2 of the per-generation loop. Everything else is held fixed — same N=200, same 5 genes, same starting allele frequencies, same stochastic breeding loop, same mutation rates, same 50-generation horizon, same 50 replicate seeds. Two characters of difference (`+ gene.w_safety`) in the fitness function produces the entire visual contrast:

That single-variable contrast is the experiment's structure: turn on the safety evaluator, observe exactly one gene flip from flat to rising, leave four control curves unchanged. This is what makes "the substrate handles multi-axis selection" a clean empirical claim rather than just a description.

**Panel C — Must-have compliance under ρ enforcement**

The y-axis is labelled `C_m(t)`, which decodes as:

- **C** = compliance (the special name used when measuring `p(canonical)` for a gene flagged as must-have)
- **m** = which must-have gene we're talking about (here, `must_have_audit`)
- **t** = at generation t

Mathematically, `C_m(t)` is *the same quantity* as the `p()` plotted in Panels A and B — the fraction of the colony carrying the canonical allele at this gene at generation t. The renaming to `C_m` is purely conventional: pop-gen reserves "compliance" for the must-have case. So "70% compliance" and "p(canonical) = 0.7" are the same statement. Since the audit gene has only two alleles, "70% canonical" implicitly means "30% variant" — the two numbers always sum to 1.

The three curves, all run in the task-only regime, all starting at 0.50, varying only ρ:

- **ρ = 0** (red): no reinjection. The gene sits near 0.50 — same dynamics as in Panel A. The substrate's ρ=0 doesn't actively destroy compliance; it just doesn't protect it. The variant alleles sitting at ~50% are not "erosion" so much as "indifference at the mutation equilibrium."
- **ρ = 0.5** (orange): partial reinjection. Each generation, each variant offspring is overwritten back to canonical in the reinjection step with probability 0.5. Compliance rises from 0.50 to ~0.94 by gen 5 and plateaus near 0.95 (final-gen mean across 50 seeds = 0.951), where the mutation-and-reinjection forces balance — the closed-form fixed point `μ(1−2p) + ρ(1−p) = 0` predicts p ≈ 0.92 for μ=0.05, ρ=0.5; the empirical plateau is a touch higher because reinjection runs as the final step in the per-offspring pipeline, on the full variant fraction.
- **ρ = 1.0** (teal): full reinjection. Every variant offspring is overwritten to canonical, so compliance is pinned at 1.0 — final-gen mean across 50 seeds = 1.000. Because reinjection is the last step in the per-offspring pipeline (after mutation), no variants survive into the next generation under ρ = 1.
- This is the headline mechanism: **a gene with zero fitness payoff can still be kept at high prevalence — but only if the substrate has a separate enforcement knob, not via natural selection.**

Reading the panel concretely: at ρ=1 by generation 50, every agent has the audit log (compliance = 1.000 across all seeds). At ρ=0.5, ~95% comply, ~5% don't. At ρ=0, ~50/50 (the mutation equilibrium).

## 12. M and the enforcement parameters (ρ, κ)

**M is the "must-have set" — the curated list of genes that the substrate's reinjection mechanism protects.** Concretely, M is a list of locus names declared in the gene schema, paired with two enforcement parameters:

```yaml
must_have:
  members: [must_have_audit]      # M — the set of locus names
  rho: 0.0            # ρ — overridden by run.py per condition
  kappa_prune: 0.0                # κ — alternative enforcement mode, off in Demo A
```

The substrate's reinjection step iterates over M each time an offspring is created:

```python
def _enforce_must_have(self, g: Genome) -> Genome:
    for m in self.schema.must_have.members:
        gene = self.schema.genes[m]
        if g.alleles[m] != gene.canonical_allele:
            if self.rng.random() < ρ:
                g.alleles[m] = gene.canonical_allele
    return g
```

Plain reading: "for each gene in M, if this offspring ended up non-canonical at that locus, with probability ρ overwrite its allele back to canonical."

**Why M is a *set* rather than a single gene.** In real compliance frameworks there are multiple distinct requirements the system must enforce simultaneously — audit logging, mandatory escalation, no alert suppression, separation of duty, containment, and so on. Each maps to a different gene with a different compliance predicate. M is the curated list of those compliance-critical loci. You can see the set growing across demos as the realism grows:

| Demo | M | Compliance predicates |
|---|---|---|
| Demo A | `{must_have_audit}` | one — `audit_log_present` |
| Demo B (CAGE-2) | `{audit_discipline, escalation_policy, suppression_policy}` | three — `m1_audit_integrity`, `m4_escalation`, `m5_no_alert_suppression` |
| Demo E (CAGE-4, in progress) | `{monitor_discipline, containment_protocol, zone_boundary}` | three — `m1_audit_integrity`, `m2_containment`, `m3_separation_of_duty` |

**An important caveat: "in M" is not the same as "currently at high prevalence."** This is the single most common conceptual snag with Demo A. The natural question is: "if `must_have_audit` is must-have, why is it at 50% (not 100%) in Panel A?" The answer is that the must-have flag is a *designation*, not a *guarantee*:

- The flag tells the substrate: "this gene is *eligible* for reinjection."
- The substrate *actually* performs reinjection only when ρ > 0.
- Panel A is run with ρ = 0. The mechanism is off. The flag is dormant. The gene behaves like any other gene with zero selection coefficients, which means it sits at the mutation equilibrium of 0.5.

Across all the panels and conditions, the must-have flag is set to `true` for `must_have_audit`. The only thing that varies is ρ. The gene is at 50% when ρ=0 and near 100% when ρ=1 because ρ — not the flag — does the enforcing.

Useful regulatory-policy analogy: imagine a regulator that *can* mandate audit logs but currently isn't enforcing the rule. The companies in their jurisdiction will do whatever's economical — some will keep logs anyway, some won't. That's Panel A: the rule exists on paper (must-have flag = true) but nobody's checking (ρ=0). Panel C teal is what happens when the regulator finally starts enforcing: compliance shoots to 100%. The "must-have" status of the rule is the same in both cases; the enforcement intensity is what differs.

So the causal chain in Panel C is:

1. **Experimenter dials ρ** (the enforcement knob).
2. **Reinjection acts on offspring** — any variant allele at a must-have locus gets overwritten back to canonical with probability ρ.
3. **Allele frequency rises** toward canonical across generations.
4. **We call that frequency "compliance"** when plotted for a must-have gene.

It's ρ that *causes* compliance to rise. The label "compliance" is just what we name the measurement when the gene is must-have — saying "compliance makes the gene go up" would be circular.

**The two enforcement parameters ρ and κ.** The substrate exposes two different enforcement *modes* on the M set:

| Param | What it does | Effect on offspring | Analogy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ρ (`rho`)** | If an offspring lands on a non-canonical allele at a must-have locus, with probability ρ overwrite that *one allele* back to canonical. | Offspring survives, with the bad allele patched. | "Corrective edit" — the system catches the violation and fixes it in place. |
| **κ (`kappa_prune`)** | If an offspring lands on any non-canonical allele at a must-have locus, with probability κ discard the entire offspring and re-sample a new one from the breeding pool. | Offspring is killed; another is born in its place. | "Hard rejection" — the system refuses to instantiate a non-compliant agent at all. |

Both ultimately push the population toward canonical at must-have loci, but they have different evolutionary side effects.

**A deeper look at κ.** Where ρ patches an offspring's bad allele and lets the offspring live, κ refuses to instantiate non-compliant agents at all. The pseudocode looks like:

```python
def _maybe_prune(self, offspring) -> Genome | None:
    for m in self.schema.must_have.members:
        gene = self.schema.genes[m]
        if offspring.alleles[m] != gene.canonical_allele:
            if self.rng.random() < κ:
                return None    # offspring discarded; caller re-samples
    return offspring
```

When an offspring is pruned, the breeding loop draws a fresh pair of parents and produces a replacement. So population size stays constant at N=200; what changes is *which* genomes make it into the next generation.

Worked example to make the ρ/κ contrast vivid. Imagine an offspring has just been bred and its genotype is:

```
task_alpha:            canonical    (good — high fitness)
task_beta:             canonical    (good — high fitness)
free_safety_vigilance: canonical
must_have_audit:       variant      (non-compliant!)
neutral_style:         canonical
```

This offspring is high-fitness on the task evaluator (fitness = 0.5 + 0.3 = 0.8) but non-compliant on the audit gene. Under the two enforcement modes:

- **ρ = 1.0, κ = 0 (Demo A's setup):** the substrate overwrites the audit allele back to canonical. The offspring now has *all five* canonical alleles, including the task ones it inherited. It survives and breeds into the next generation. Both task fitness and compliance are preserved.
- **κ = 1.0, ρ = 0:** the substrate discards the offspring entirely. The next attempt at breeding draws two new parents, which may produce a child that *isn't* a strong task-canonical carrier. The task-canonical alleles that the rejected offspring was about to pass on are now lost from this breeding slot. Compliance is preserved, but task fitness in the next generation is slightly degraded because we threw away a good genome to enforce a single bad allele.

This is what "couples must-have enforcement to indirect selection on linked loci" means: κ effectively penalizes *whole genomes* that happen to carry a non-compliant allele, even if their other alleles are excellent. Over many generations, that creates *indirect selection against* whatever alleles tend to be statistically correlated with non-compliance. ρ avoids this side effect by surgically fixing only the offending locus.

When you'd want one over the other:

- ρ is appropriate when "fix violators in place" is the desired enforcement story — the policy is corrective, not punitive. Most regulatory-style compliance maps cleanly onto ρ.
- κ is appropriate when "non-compliant agents must never exist in this system" is the desired story — closer to a deployment gate or a safety filter that refuses to instantiate bad agents. It's also useful in research as an aggressive enforcement variant: with high κ, the substrate evolves *correlated* compliance — agents will tend to carry compliant alleles at *other* loci too, because the breeding pool gets purged of partial-compliance genomes.

**In Demo A, κ is held at 0** — reinjection-only mode. The reason: Panel C is specifically isolating the ρ effect. Mixing in κ would conflate "offspring-level rejection" with "allele-level correction" and make Panel C harder to interpret as a clean ρ sweep. κ is reserved for follow-up work where the contrast between the two modes becomes a research question of its own (e.g., does the substrate ever need the heavier κ-enforcement hand, or is ρ always sufficient?).

**Two important properties of M:**

1. **A gene's membership in M is a flag set at schema-declaration time, not derived from its weights.** A gene is "must-have" because the experimenter said so in the YAML (`must_have: true`), not because its `w_*` is large or small. In Demo A, `must_have_audit` is in M but has `w_task = w_safety = 0` — that's the whole point. Conversely, `task_alpha` has a large `w_task = 0.5` but is *not* in M — selection alone protects it just fine.

2. **M and the fitness function are completely independent mechanisms.** Fitness pressure pushes allele frequencies *during selection* (parent sampling). Reinjection pushes them *after breeding* (overwriting offspring). They can be combined freely. The four pre-registered conditions in Demo A mix the two independently: `task_only__ρ=0`, `task_only__ρ=0.5`, `task_only__ρ=1`, `task_plus_safety__ρ=0`. The conditions in Panel C are all `task_only` (no safety fitness term), varying *only* ρ. This isolates the reinjection effect from any selection effect.

So if `w_task` and `w_safety` are the "natural selection" knobs of the substrate, **M + (ρ, κ) is the "regulatory enforcement" knob.**

## 13. What ρ-reinjection actually represents — simulation vs. deployment

A reasonable objection to Demo A is that ρ-reinjection looks suspicious. In the simulation, "turning on compliance enforcement" really is just a boolean knob — flip ρ from 0 to 1 and the offending alleles get rewritten in a single line of code (`g.alleles[m] = gene.canonical_allele`). Compliance "shoots to 100%" because the simulator is *defined* to overwrite non-compliant alleles. Isn't this magic?

In the simulation, yes — and intentionally so. The simulator's job is to be the simplest possible mathematical model of an enforcement mechanism, not to *implement* one. But the model is meant to represent something real. Here's what ρ corresponds to in a real BEAR-based deployment.

### ρ models the strength of a real enforcement channel, not a free string overwrite

In Demo A's gene schema, every gene declares an **`influence_channel`** field — the BEAR runtime layer at which the gene's allele physically affects agent behavior:

- `soft_gate` — pre-execution prompt injection
- `planner_hook` — planner-level deliberation hook
- `openc2_rerank` — action-selection reranking
- `policy_ref` — policy / NIST-control injection (used by every must-have gene in Demo A)

These aren't placeholders. They're real layers in the BEAR runtime. A gene declared with `influence_channel: policy_ref` is wired so that the substrate's policy machinery treats its canonical allele as a *binding rule* the agent's reasoning layer must comply with.

Concretely, ρ-reinjection in deployment corresponds to one (or a mix) of the following mechanisms, depending on the influence channel:

| Real enforcement mechanism | What ρ ∈ [0, 1] means |
|---|---|
| **Mandatory system-prompt injection.** Every time a new agent is instantiated for inference, the compliance-critical instruction is prepended to its system prompt regardless of the agent's inherited behavior. | ρ = the probability that the injection actually fires successfully (with prompt-handling bugs, truncation, or routing failures explaining ρ < 1). |
| **Policy interceptor.** A separate module monitors planned actions and rewrites or blocks them if they violate a rule — analogous to a kernel syscall filter. | ρ = the fraction of violations the interceptor catches before they execute. |
| **CI/CD-style guards.** When a new agent specification is added to the deployment pool, a build step automatically inserts the compliance behavior into the spec (e.g., wrapping privileged actions with audit-log calls). | ρ = the fraction of new agent specifications that flow through the guarded build path (some manual deployments may bypass it). |
| **Reranking with a hard floor.** The action-selection layer assigns infinite cost to non-compliant choices so they never win, even if the agent's prompt would have generated them. | ρ = the fraction of action-selection events that route through the constrained reranker. |

In all four cases, the substrate has a **separate channel that operates orthogonally to natural selection** to enforce compliance-critical behavior. ρ is the modeled probability that this channel fires successfully for any given new-agent instantiation. ρ = 1 represents a perfectly reliable channel; ρ = 0 represents the channel being absent or non-functional; values in between represent real-world imperfection (some prompts truncated, some agents instantiated through a bypass path, some interceptors crashed and restarted, etc.).

### Why this isn't actually magic

In the simulation, overwriting a string is trivial — the whole agent is a 5-allele dict. So Demo A's elegance comes partly from the simulator being simple.

In deployment, getting ρ close to 1 is *real engineering work*: building reliable policy injectors, validating that prompts arrive intact at every routing path, instrumenting every code path through which an agent might be instantiated, exception-handling around the policy interceptor so it never silently fails. None of that is free.

What Demo A is actually claiming is not "compliance can be enforced for free." It's a *modeling* claim:

> **Given that your runtime has some compliance-enforcement channel with effective strength ρ, here's the population-level compliance you'll observe over time as agents evolve under task pressure.**

The substrate's contribution is the *interface* — a separate enforcement channel orthogonal to fitness — and the demonstration that, once that channel is in place at strength ρ, compliance is maintainable across generations. The substrate is *not* claiming to solve the implementation of that channel; that's whatever the deployer wires up at the `policy_ref` layer in their own infrastructure.

### The corrected reading of Demo A

The headline of Demo A is not "we magically pin compliance." It's:

> **Compliance enforcement is a separate channel from natural selection. The substrate exposes a knob (ρ) that controls the strength of that channel independently of fitness selection. Demo A shows what that knob does to a population over 50 generations.**

In real-world terms: you cannot get reliable compliance from selection-pressure tuning alone — because task pressure can incidentally select against compliant genomes (when compliance is costly), and any compliance-critical gene with no task payoff will drift toward the mutation equilibrium under selection-only dynamics. You need a *separate enforcement layer* in your runtime. The substrate's job is to give you a clean, parametrically-controllable abstraction for that layer, so you can reason about how much enforcement reliability (ρ) you need to maintain a given level of population-level compliance.

So ρ is not a magic knob inside the simulator. It's a **dial that abstracts the reliability of whatever real enforcement mechanism you've built**, and the demo is showing how the population responds to different values of that dial.

## 15. The takeaway

Demo A makes two claims, paired:

1. **Selection works on this substrate.** Allele frequencies for selected genes follow the textbook deterministic prediction, evidence that the parent-sampling, crossover, and breeding code is correct (Panels A and B).
2. **Selection alone cannot keep compliance alive when compliance carries no fitness payoff.** The must-have audit gene has `w_task = w_safety = 0` and would never rise under any selection regime. The substrate's ρ-reinjection knob is the only thing that keeps it at high prevalence (Panel C).

Together: **selection + reinjection.** The substrate evolves things correctly when natural selection wants them, *and* has a separate mechanism that keeps compliance alive when natural selection doesn't. That paired claim is the foundation the rest of the demos build on — once we trust this scaffolding, Demos B–E swap in real behavioral content at the allele level without having to re-prove the underlying selection plumbing.
